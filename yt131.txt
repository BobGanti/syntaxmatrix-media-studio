
Now, some people will say: “But these models are dangerous. Of course access must be restricted.”

And again, there is truth in that.

But let us not pretend restriction has only one purpose.

Restriction can protect society.

But restriction can also protect market power.

Restriction can protect safety.

But restriction can also protect business models.

Restriction can prevent misuse.

But restriction can also prevent competition.

Restriction can reduce risk.

But restriction can also concentrate power.

And when the same companies that speak about democratizing intelligence also keep the strongest intelligence behind limited access programs, we have to ask whether the long-term structure of AI is becoming too centralized.

This is the key point.

I am not saying safety is fake.

I am saying safety must not become the permanent excuse for a world where only a few organizations are allowed to use the most powerful tools.

Because if that happens, then AI does not democratize intelligence.

It privatizes intelligence.

It turns intelligence into a rented service.

It turns developers into dependent tenants.

It turns companies into API dependents.

It turns governments into customers of private intelligence infrastructure.

It turns universities and small labs into spectators.

And it turns the rest of the world into people waiting for permission.

That is not a healthy future.

And this is exactly why responsible open-source AI matters.

Not reckless open source.

Not irresponsible release.

Not dumping powerful systems into the wild with no evaluation.
