Closed AI, Gatekeeping, and Why Open Source Now Matters

There is something happening in artificial intelligence right now that we need to talk about honestly.

And I want to be very clear from the beginning.

This is not an anti-OpenAI message.

This is not an anti-Anthropic message.

This is not even an anti-closed-model message.

Closed frontier models are important. They are pushing the boundary of what artificial intelligence can do. They are advancing coding, cybersecurity, scientific reasoning, agentic workflows, and many other areas.

But there is a deeper issue emerging.

And that issue is access.

Who gets access to the most powerful intelligence?

Who decides who is trusted?

Who waits outside the gate?

And what happens when the future of software, research, cybersecurity, business automation, education, and national productivity depends on tools controlled by only a few private companies?

That is the question we need to start asking.

Because when you look at what is happening with GPT-5.6 Sol and Claude Mythos 5, you begin to see a pattern.

OpenAI says GPT-5.6 Sol is one of its strongest models yet, especially in areas like coding, science, agentic reasoning, biology, and cybersecurity.

Anthropic says Claude Mythos 5 is built for high-end cybersecurity work and later biology research.

These are not ordinary chatbots.

These are frontier systems.

These are models that can reason deeply, write code, analyze vulnerabilities, assist with complex workflows, and potentially change how entire industries operate.

So naturally, the companies say they must be careful.

And to be fair, they are not wrong.

Safety matters.

When a model becomes powerful enough to help with cybersecurity, vulnerability discovery, autonomous coding, scientific workflows, and high-stakes decision support, you cannot release it recklessly.

Nobody serious should argue for careless release.

Nobody serious should say every powerful model should be thrown onto the internet with no evaluation, no safeguards, no governance, and no accountability.

That is not my argument.

My argument is different.

My question is this:

When does safety become gatekeeping?

When does responsible deployment become selective privilege?

When does “trusted access” become a closed intelligence economy?
################################################################################111
Because right now, the public message from the frontier labs is usually about public benefit, safety, democratizing intelligence, helping humanity, empowering developers, empowering researchers, and making AI broadly useful.

But when the most powerful models arrive, the first access often goes to selected partners, approved organizations, large companies, critical infrastructure operators, government-visible circles, and institutions that already have strong relationships with the labs.

The public waits.

Independent developers wait.

Small companies wait.

Universities wait.

Researchers outside the chosen networks wait.

Builders in smaller markets wait.

Startups wait.

Countries without special access wait.

And this is where the contradiction begins.

If artificial intelligence is going to become the operating system of the modern world, then access to intelligence cannot be controlled only by a small number of private companies and their approved partner networks.

Because this is no longer just about who gets a better chatbot.

This is about who gets better coding ability.

Who gets better cybersecurity capability.

Who gets better research tools.

Who gets better automation.

Who gets better scientific assistance.

Who gets better productivity.

Who gets better national competitiveness.

Who gets the ability to build faster, defend faster, discover faster, and learn faster.

That is why this matters.
