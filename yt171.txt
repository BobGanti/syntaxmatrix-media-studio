
So when we look at GPT-5.6 Sol and Claude Mythos 5, we should not only ask which model is stronger.

We should not only ask which benchmark is better.

We should not only ask whether one beats the other in coding, cybersecurity, or reasoning.

We should ask a bigger question:

What kind of AI future are we building?

A future where intelligence is centralized, permissioned, rented, and restricted?

Or a future where powerful AI exists within a broader ecosystem of open, auditable, self-hostable, responsibly governed alternatives?

That is the real debate.

And my position is simple.

The answer to dangerous AI cannot be permanent closed control.

The answer to dangerous AI must be responsible openness.

Independent auditing.

Transparent evaluations.

Clear governance.

Secure deployment.

Strong monitoring where needed.

Enterprise-grade controls.

Human accountability.

And serious open-source alternatives that give developers and organizations the ability to inspect, adapt, and govern the systems they depend on.

Because if intelligence becomes the infrastructure layer of the twenty-first century, then access to that intelligence cannot belong only to the few.

It must not become a privilege reserved for insiders.

It must not become a tool available only to those approved by the biggest labs and the strongest governments.

It must not become a private operating system for the world.

That is why open-source AI now matters.

Not because it is perfect.

Not because it is automatically safe.

Not because every model should be released without limits.
